About Me

I am a public health and data analyst with a strong interest in how evidence, policy, and operational decision-making intersect to shape health outcomes. I completed a Master of Public Health at Imperial College London, graduating with a distinction and specialising in Health Promotion and Service Improvement.

My academic work focused on the wider determinants of health, particularly the role of commercial, structural, and environmental factors in shaping non-communicable disease risk across the life course. My dissertation explored the relationship between public transport infrastructure and cardiometabolic health outcomes, combining quantitative analysis with policy interpretation. I presented this work to academic and non-academic audiences and was later invited back to present the findings to new students and staff.

What motivates my work is the belief that good data should lead to better decisions, especially where resources are limited and inequalities are entrenched. I am particularly interested in roles that sit between analysis, policy, and service improvement, where insight can directly inform action.

Career & Professional Experience

Before moving fully into public health and analytics, I worked in healthcare workforce and recruitment roles that operated in close partnership with NHS trusts and independent providers. My work focused on supplying doctors and nurses into frontline services, often during periods of significant system pressure.

I supported workforce delivery through major periods of change, including Covid-19, Brexit-related immigration shifts, and the implementation of GDPR. This required managing uncertainty, translating complex regulatory requirements into operational processes, and communicating clearly with clinicians, managers, and external stakeholders.

These roles developed my ability to prioritise competing demands, work with incomplete information, and communicate with impact. I regularly used structured approaches such as the Eisenhower Matrix for prioritisation and the Situation, Behaviour, Impact framework to support effective communication and feedback.

This background informs how I approach data analysis today. I am less interested in analysis for its own sake, and more focused on producing insight that is timely, relevant, and genuinely useful for people making decisions in complex systems.
